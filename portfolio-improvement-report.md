# Portfolio Improvement Report: daveross858.com vs. Industry Leaders

**Researched:** Melanie Lantin (VP, Warner Bros. Discovery), Joann Wu (VP, Uber), Jason Day (SVP, Salesforce), Edouard Picot (VP, Salesforce), Breanne Rotelli (VP, Meta), Jaladhi Pujara (VP, Braze), Shantanu Garud (VP, TriNet), and reference portfolios (buzzusborne.com, casestudy.club Top 20, UX Playbook 2026 senior guide).

---

## 🔴 Critical — Do These First

### 1. Replace the thumbnail wall with 3–5 deep case studies
**What leaders do:** Every top-ranked portfolio (Buzz Usborne, Melanie Lantin, Simon Pan) shows 3–5 curated projects with full process narrative — not a grid of 10 thumbnails. Melanie Lantin's "Magnolia Interactive Map" case study includes a video demo, her leadership role, the research process, technical decisions, and feature details. Buzz Usborne has 10 case studies but each has a compelling one-line hook and full write-up.

**What Dave's site does:** Shows 10 thumbnail cards. Clicking any of them goes to `#!projects/*.html` — stub pages or nothing. There is no process story, no research narrative, no outcome data, no "here's what I was trying to solve."

**Fix:** Pick your 3 best projects (suggest: Text Time Machine, Hey!, and Participate or Air Co.). Build a real case study page for each with: problem statement → your role → research/process → key decisions → outcome + metrics. The other 7 can stay as a secondary grid labeled "More work."

---

### 2. Add real, quantified outcomes to every featured project
**What leaders do:** The 2026 hiring standard for senior UX is "show business impact." Phrases like "reduced checkout abandonment 18%," "launched to 6M users," or "earned Apple Best in Class award" (Melanie Lantin literally has this) are what differentiate a senior hire from a mid-level one.

**What Dave's site does:** The one-liners added in the redesign are placeholders — "increased session time 40%" (Participate) is not attributed to a real outcome. Hiring managers at VP-level companies will fact-check or ask directly in interviews.

**Fix:** Go through each project and pull one real metric: user count, revenue, NPS change, retention impact, team size led, timeline compressed. Even "launched on time with a team of 4" is a real data point. Replace every fictional stat with a real one, or remove the claim.

---

### 3. Rewrite the About section as an executive leadership narrative
**What leaders do:** Melanie Lantin's About is not "I'm a designer." It reads: *"Build and scale high-performing hybrid UX organizations… Known for cultivating strong team culture… Lead with a product-first, customer-obsessed mindset… Proven track record of transforming teams into agile, design-led organizations."* She leads with what she makes happen, not what tools she uses.

**What Dave's site does:** The About section (not rewritten in the redesign) reads as a personal bio — years of experience, tools used, personality summary. This is the right frame for a mid-level designer, not someone targeting Director/VP/Lead roles.

**Fix:** Rewrite About around three leadership competencies, e.g.: (1) How you build and ship products, (2) How you lead teams or influence cross-functional work, (3) What you uniquely bring to an org. Add 1 sentence of personality at the end. Drop the tool list entirely — your case studies demonstrate those.

## 🟡 High Impact — Do These Next

### 4. Add a Thought Leadership section (talks, articles, or writing)
**What leaders do:** Melanie Lantin has a full "Speaker Series" page listing every talk, audience size, and conference. Jason Day (@JasonDay_UX) actively posts UX leadership content on X. Joann Wu shares Uber design work and team events. Buzz Usborne has "Writing," "Press," and "Resources" pages.

**What Dave's site does:** Nothing. No articles, no talks, no posts, no published opinions.

**Fix:** Even 2–3 things qualify: a LinkedIn article, a conference talk, a guest post, a Dribbble shot that got traction, an open-source project. Create a simple "Writing & Speaking" section. If nothing exists yet, write one LinkedIn article about a UX problem you've solved — then link it here. Thought leadership is a multiplier for job hunting; it signals you're not just doing the work, you're shaping the field.

---

### 5. Sharpen the hero positioning statement
**What leaders do:** Every great portfolio leads with a sharp, specific claim. Melanie: *"USER EXPERIENCE & PRODUCT DESIGN EXECUTIVE LEADER."* Buzz: *"Drawing boxes since '05"* (personality-forward). Simon Pan: known for Uber Magic 2.0 and Amazon Prime Music case studies — his name IS the positioning.

**What Dave's site does:** "David W. Ross" + role list + location. This is a LinkedIn headline, not a positioning statement. The "Open to work" badge is good signal, but there's no sharp sentence that answers "why Dave specifically."

**Fix:** Write one sentence under your name that captures your unique angle. Options: *"I build products people use, and teams that keep building them"* or *"UX engineer + product strategist — I work at the intersection of design and code"* or *"15 years shipping consumer + enterprise products from 0 to 1."* One sentence. Make it yours.

---

### 6. Add social proof (testimonials or recognitions)
**What leaders do:** Melanie Lantin prominently features her Apple "Best in Class" award and her Emmy nomination for The Voice App. Joann Wu's career press coverage (TechCrunch on her Uber hire) is discoverable. Leaders' LinkedIn profiles are full of recommendations.

**What Dave's site does:** Zero social proof. No awards, no quotes, no press, no recognitions.

**Fix:** Pull 2–3 strong LinkedIn recommendations and display them as short blockquote testimonials. Add any awards, mentions, or recognitions (a "Featured on…" badge, an app store rating, a client quote). Even a simple *"Recommended by [Name], Director of Product at [Company]"* adds significant credibility.

---

### 7. Make your hero photo bigger and more confident
**What leaders do:** Leader portfolios either go photo-free (work is the face) or use a large, commanding, professionally shot headshot. Melanie Lantin uses no photo on her work pages — her name and title carry the authority. Buzz Usborne goes photo-free entirely.

**What Dave's site does:** 120px circular avatar — effectively a profile picture. At this size it reads as a LinkedIn thumbnail, not a confident leadership statement.

**Fix:** Either (a) go significantly larger — 200–250px minimum, full-bleed if possible — with a professional photo taken in a neutral/studio setting, or (b) remove the photo from the hero entirely and let the text/work stand alone. The current size is the worst of both options.

## 🟢 Polish — Round Out the Experience

### 8. Fix the résumé download CTA
The "Download résumé" button in the hero CTA currently links to `#`. This is a broken promise — every recruiter who clicks it and gets nothing is a lost opportunity. Either link a real, current PDF (`/assets/david-ross-resume.pdf`) or remove the button until the file is ready.

### 9. Remove the skills section or collapse it into context
**What the best portfolios do:** Skills are never listed in isolation. They're demonstrated through case studies. Buzz Usborne has no skills section. Melanie Lantin has no skills section. The UX Playbook 2026 guide explicitly says "a skills list is the least interesting thing on your portfolio."

**What Dave's site does:** The redesigned chip cards are cleaner than the old % circles, but 25+ tech chips across 3 cards is still a "skills dump." It tells hiring managers what tools you know, not what you can do.

**Fix:** Collapse skills to one sentence in the About section: *"I work across the full stack — Figma to React, research to roadmap."* Let each case study demonstrate the specific tools used in context. Remove the standalone Skills section.

### 10. Add Open Graph / social preview meta tags
When Dave's URL is shared in Slack, a recruiter's email, or LinkedIn, it currently shows a blank preview. All professional portfolios have a compelling social preview card with a strong og:image (typically your hero photo or a project screenshot), your name, and your positioning statement.

**Fix:** Add to the `<head>`:
```html
<meta property="og:title" content="David W. Ross — UX Engineer & Product Designer">
<meta property="og:description" content="15 years shipping consumer and enterprise products. Open to new opportunities in the SF Bay Area.">
<meta property="og:image" content="https://daveross858.com/images/og-preview.jpg">
<meta property="og:url" content="https://daveross858.com">
<meta name="twitter:card" content="summary_large_image">
```

### 11. Clarify the portfolio filter logic
The current filter tabs (Web Design / UI Design / UX Design / Product) overlap heavily — most projects are tagged in 3 categories. This isn't meaningful to a hiring manager scanning in 60 seconds.

**Fix:** Consolidate to 2–3 non-overlapping filters that map to actual job types: **Product Design**, **UI Engineering**, **Product Strategy** — or skip filters entirely and just label each case study prominently.

### 12. Add a "Currently open to" line near the contact form
The "Open to work" pulse badge is good. But nowhere does the site say *what* Dave is open to. A recruiter can't tell if he's targeting IC roles, lead roles, contract, full-time, SF Bay only, or remote.

**Fix:** Add one line near the contact section or in the hero: *"Currently open to Senior UX / Product Design Lead roles — Bay Area or remote."* This dramatically improves response rate from recruiters.
